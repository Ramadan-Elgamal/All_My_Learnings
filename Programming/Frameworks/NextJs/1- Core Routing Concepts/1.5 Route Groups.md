# Route Groups
Pull up a chair. Up until now, I’ve been drilling a very strict rule into your head: **Folders dictate your URLs.** If you make a folder, you make a URL segment. Period.
But what happens when that strict rule actually gets in our way? Let's talk about how to break it intentionally.
## The "Why": The Organization vs. URL Conflict
Imagine our app is growing fast. We have a login page, a registration page, and a forgot-password page. Naturally, as an engineer, you want to keep your codebase clean. You decide to group them all inside a folder called auth.
Following Next.js rules, your paths now look like this:
 * app/auth/login/page.tsx -> your-site.com/auth/login
 * app/auth/register/page.tsx -> your-site.com/auth/register
But then the marketing team comes to your desk. They say, *"Hey, we want the login page to just be your-site.com/login. It converts better. Get rid of the /auth part."*
In a traditional setup, you have a dilemma. You either have to pull all those files out of your nice, neat auth folder and dump them loosely into the root app directory (making a mess), or you tell marketing no.
Next.js gives us a third option: **Route Groups**.
## The Mental Model: Silent Letters
Think of Route Groups like silent letters in an English word. When you spell the word "Knight", the "K" is there on the paper (your folder structure). But when you speak the word out loud (the URL), you completely ignore the "K".
To create a Route Group, you simply wrap a folder's name in parentheses.
If you name your folder (auth) instead of auth, Next.js will use it for internal organization but will completely ignore it when generating the public URL.
## Building Incrementally: Seeing the Paths
Let's look at how wrapping a folder in parentheses changes our routing tree:
 * **Standard Folder:** app/auth/login/page.tsx equals /auth/login
 * **Route Group:** app/(auth)/login/page.tsx equals /login
The (auth) folder acts purely as a filing cabinet for you, the developer. The browser has no idea it exists.
## Teaching Patterns: Opting Out of Layouts
Keeping folders organized is nice, but Route Groups have a much more powerful architectural use case: **Layout management.**
Remember how Layouts wrap everything inside them? If you put a layout.tsx at the root of your app directory, it forces *every single page* in your app to share that layout. But what if your main application needs a heavy sidebar navigation, but your marketing landing page and your login screen need to be completely blank, full-screen pages?
You use Route Groups to split your app into separate "zones".
Instead of one giant root layout, you can structure your app like this:
 1. app/(marketing)/layout.tsx (Has a promotional header)
 2. app/(marketing)/page.tsx -> URL is /
 3. app/(dashboard)/layout.tsx (Has the complex sidebar)
 4. app/(dashboard)/settings/page.tsx -> URL is /settings
Because Route Groups don't affect the URL, you successfully created entirely different layout wrappers for different parts of your site, while keeping your URLs exactly how you want them.
## Mistakes to Avoid Before You Make Them
 * **Mistake 1: Creating URL Conflicts (The Collision Bug).**
   * *Reality:* Because Route Groups are invisible to the URL, you can accidentally create two identical routes. If you have app/(marketing)/about/page.tsx and app/(shop)/about/page.tsx, Next.js will crash during the build. Both routes resolve to /about, and Next.js won't know which one to show the user.
 * **Mistake 2: Overusing Route Groups.**
   * *Reality:* Don't wrap every single folder in parentheses just because you can. If having /shop/shoes makes logical sense for your users, just use a normal shop folder. Route Groups are an organizational escape hatch, not the default way to build apps.
## Practical Exercise
Let's clean up our project using a Route Group.
 1. In your app folder, you currently have that admin folder we built earlier.
 2. Let's say we don't want "admin" in the URL anymore, but we want to keep the folder for our own sanity.
 3. Right-click the admin folder and rename it to (admin).
 4. Check your terminal where your Next.js server is running. You might see it recompile.
 5. Go to your browser and try to visit http://localhost:3000/admin. You will get a 404 Error! The path no longer exists.
 6. Now, change the URL to just http://localhost:3000/. You will see the Admin Home page content (because (admin)/page.tsx now resolves to the root!).
 7. Visit http://localhost:3000/users. You will see your User Management page, still wrapped perfectly in the admin layout, but with a much cleaner URL.
